For the majority of the 1980s, Nintendo dominated the video game market with its 8-bit NES console after it revived the gaming industry following the crash of 1983. The company paved the way for quality, hand-crafted games rather than the constant flood of shovelware being put out before Nintendo took over as the dominating company.

However, in 1988, Nintendo started to form a rivalry with company Sega, who put out a 16-bit machine, the Sega Genesis. The graphics on the Genesis looked better than anything Nintendo could've possibly put out on their flagship NES console. The Genesis was faster, sleeker, and was the hot new item of the late 80s and early 90s. It single-handedly beat down the giant that Nintendo was, and Sega was beginning to take over the spotlight.

Enter: The Super Nintendo Entertainment System

This was Nintendo's response to Sega--their first 16-bit console. It debuted strong in North America in 1991, selling out across stores everywhere. It was the only way to play games that took advantage of Mode 7 technology, which allowed for breath-taking pseudo-3D graphics, making launch games Pilotwings and F-Zero must-have titles of that holiday season.

The Super Nintendo had quite a few benefits over the Sega Genesis. Some of these include:

  • Double the RAM (128 KB vs 64 KB)
  • Eight times the sound RAM (64 KB vs 8 KB)
  • Four times the amount of on-screen colours (256 vs 64)
  • Two more audio channels (8 vs 6)
